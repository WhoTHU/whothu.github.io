Reinforcement learning (RL) is usually described as a technical problem: an agent interacts with an environment, observes a **state**, takes an **action**, and receives a **reward**. Over time, it learns a **policy** that maximizes long-term return.

It maps surprisingly well onto life. We also move through the world by making sequential decisions. Every major choice — what to study, where to live, what work to pursue, what to give up — is an **action**. Our abilities, relationships, property form the **state**. We have a **policy** that guides our choices to obtain **rewards**, and we update it over time based on feedback. 

So in one sense, life does look like a reinforcement learning problem.

### Part I. Life looks like a typical RL problem

1. Life is sequential decision-making

    The value of a life choice is rarely just its immediate outcome. A decision changes your future state, and also changes your future options. Choosing a field, a city, a partner today affects the action space tomorrow.

    That is exactly the structure RL is built for.

2. Life have some reward

    People are typically optimizing for something: meaning, achievement, money, status, love, freedom, peace, stability, or some mixture of them. The reward function is often hard to articulate, since it is complex, multi-dimensional, and evolves over time. But it is there.

    This is why life advice often conflicts. Different people have different reward functions. A path that is optimal for someone who values status may be terrible for someone who values autonomy. Many disagreements about “the right choice” are not about strategy, but about reward.

3. Rewards are delayed and noisy

    A lot of what matters in life has delayed payoff. Learning, discipline, health, and trust often produce weak short-term reward and strong long-term return. Meanwhile, entertainment and comfort often gain immediate rewards.

    This makes life hard in the same way long-horizon RL is hard: credit assignment is messy, and the best actions do not always feel best in the moment.

4. We learn from other people’s trajectories

    Because life is one-pass, we rely heavily on social examples. In that sense, society looks like a giant offline RL dataset: a collection of trajectories generated by other people.

### PartII. Life has a lot more than typical RL problems
5. The reward function is not prespecified

    In standard RL, the objective is specified. In life, it often is not.

    That may be the hardest part. Many people spend years optimizing goals they never consciously chose that are inherited from culture, family, or comparison. One can take a *best* policy for a given reward, but might realize later that the reward itself was not what they wanted.

    It is lucky if one can discover their own reward function early, or simply believe in a fixed one. It is often a dilemma: the more you want to figure it out, the more vague it becomes. No one knows a universal ground truth about what the reward should be. It may not be a good idea to spend too much time on finding the "true" reward. It is worth to spend some time on it, and believe in it.

6. The reward is multi-dimensional and non-additive

    The reward of a life could not be a single scalar. It could be a multiple-dimensional vector, and the dimensions are not necessarily commensurable, such that it is likely belongs to a partially ordered set. Moreover, the reward of a life is neither necessarily additive over time. 

7. Biased data and distribution shift

    Other people’s data can be useful, but may also lead directly to deeper problems. First, the data is biased by the reward functions of others. Second, it is non-stationary and full of distribution shift: the world changes faster than we can learn. A career path that worked ten years ago may break in the age of AI. Third, the available data is highly incomplete and subjects heavily to survivorship bias. We see the successful trajectories, but not the whole distribution.

8. There is no training stage

    RL agents usually get many episodes. They can fail, update, and try again. Life is a single long rollout with no cleanreset. That makes imitation tempting, mistakes costly, and uncertainty unavoidable.

### Conclusion

From the perspective of sequential decision-making, life does look like a reinforcement learning problem. We can find some insights from the RL framework, such as the importance of long-term thinking, the value of exploration, and the need to balance different reward dimensions. But life is also much more complex than a typical RL problem. The reward function is not given, thus it is not just about finding the best policy, but also about discovering and shaping the reward itself. Optimization is something we need to be careful about, as it often leads to local optima and bad exploitation. We need to be mindful of the limitations of the data we have, and the fact that we only get one shot at life.

Hope everyone can live a happy life.
